Extend knowledge

Why do good intestinal bacteria decrease?

In addition to intestinal aging and diseases, modern people’s refined diet, processed foods, high oil, salt and animal protein will also lead to insufficient prebiotics required for the growth of good bacteria, further changing the ecology of the intestinal flora.

When antibiotics are used, both good and bad bacteria may be eliminated together, disrupting intestinal balance. This is why when supplementing probiotics, you should not just look at the number of bacteria, but also whether the bacteria can really stay in the intestines.

Selected bacterial strains

Complete collection of 14 bacterial species

The first 7 species

Common bacterial species related to colonization and digestive tract function

  • Streptococcus thermophilus: Commonly found in fermented dairy products, it helps maintain digestive tract function.
  • Lactobacillus reuteri: well-studied, breaks down bile salts and participates in bacterial phase balance.
  • Bifidobacterium longum subspecies infantis: an early colonizer of the intestinal tract of infants and also commonly found in the intestinal tract of adults.
  • Bifidobacterium breve: Commonly found in the intestines of breastfed infants, it can ferment sugars.
  • Lactobacillus plantarum: survives longer in the stomach and can help maintain the balance of the flora.
  • Lactobacillus paracasei: a common LP bacteria in Taiwan, often used for daily physical adjustment.
  • Lactobacillus rhamnosus: acid-resistant and bile salt-resistant, commonly used in digestive tract care formulas.
The last 7 species

Bacteria related to colonization, metabolism and bacterial phase maintenance

  • Lactobacillus casei: often called C bacteria, has good acid resistance and intestinal colonization ability.
  • Lactobacillus fermentum: can pass gastric acid and bile salt tests and help maintain bacterial health.
  • Lactobacillus acidophilus: often colonizes the small intestine and is related to lactose metabolism and digestive tract function.
  • Lactobacillus salivarius: Found in the mouth and small intestine, resistant to gastric acid and bile salts.
  • Lactobacillus helveticus: can survive in acidic environments and participate in lactic acid production.
  • Bifidobacterium bifidum: Common in young and healthy intestines, it is one of the dominant bacteria in the large intestine.
  • Lactobacillus bulgaricus: Commonly found in yogurt and fermented dairy products, it can participate in lactose metabolism.
