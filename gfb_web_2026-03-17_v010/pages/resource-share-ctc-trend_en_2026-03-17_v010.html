Circulating Tumor Cell Detection: A new trend in precision cancer medicine

Observe CTCs directly from liquid sections, cancer cell transfer, and into the blood to understand why circulating tumor cells testing is becoming an important tool for cancer risk assessment and tracking.

All Resources News Insights Videos

Most common cancers originate from epithelial cells. When tumors continue to grow, some cancer cells will break away from the original site and enter the blood circulation, forming circulating tumor cells (CTCs). These cells are important clues to cancer metastasis and recurrence. They are also the most direct evidence in liquid sections that can be observed at the cellular level.

In the past, clinical practice mostly relied on imaging examinations to understand tumor changes. However, imaging tools may still need to be supplemented with other more sensitive information for very early, small, or dynamically changing lesions. As a result, liquid sectioning has developed rapidly. In different detection pathways such as circulating tumor cells, circulating tumor DNA, and exosomes, the value of CTC lies in the ability to "see" the cells themselves, not just speculative molecular signals.

It is not easy to find small and highly heterogeneous amounts of circulating tumor cells in the blood. The actual detection process usually includes cell enrichment, immunofluorescence staining, image acquisition, and cell identification and counting, each of which affects subsequent interpretation. This is why Good Future designed the test into a systematic process. The purpose is not to obtain a single number, but to retain cell images and marker evidence that can be interpreted later.

For subjects, the circulating tumor cells test also has the convenience of liquid slices. It can be done with a blood sample, without the need to fast beforehand or the risks of radiation exposure or contrast agents that are common with imaging tests. When these features are combined with readable cell images and report content, CTC testing is not just a single test, but an information tool that is more suitable for inclusion in risk assessment, treatment tracking and follow-up communication processes.
